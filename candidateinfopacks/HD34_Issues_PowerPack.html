District Identity

Muncie is post-industrial Indiana at its most unvarnished: a former manufacturing powerhouse reshaped by decades of factory closures, now anchored by Ball State University and IU Health Ball Memorial Hospital. Delaware County carries deep poverty, a severe opioid crisis, housing blight, and food insecurity — alongside a genuine community revitalization story being built through universities, land banks, and local philanthropy. This is a seat Democrats must hold — and the issues here are about economic dignity, safety net protection, and whether government shows up for struggling communities.

1
Food Security, SNAP Cuts & Federal Safety Net Dismantling
One in four Muncie children lives in poverty. SNAP cuts enacted in H.R. 1 are already shrinking benefits and removing people from rolls.
🔥 Immediate ImpactFederalState

Delaware County is one of Indiana's most food-insecure communities. One in four Muncie children under 18 lives in poverty and experiences food insecurity — and more than half of young adults ages 18–24 face the same. Indiana had 588,184 SNAP beneficiaries as of April 2025. The Trump administration's H.R. 1, signed July 4, 2025, enacted $187 billion in SNAP cuts nationally — reducing benefits, tightening work requirements, adding asset limits, and restricting immigration eligibility. During the 2025 government shutdown, Indiana SNAP benefits were cut to 50% for November — and Ball State researchers confirmed Delaware County food pantries immediately absorbed the overflow. Indiana also opted out of the SUN Bucks summer food program for children, leaving approximately 669,000 Hoosier school-age children without summer food assistance. By October 2026, states will shoulder 75% of SNAP administration costs — a $37M+ hit to Indiana's budget that will further squeeze the program. In Muncie, where grocery access is limited in lower-income neighborhoods, SNAP cuts also threaten local retailers whose customers depend on benefits.

2
Opioid Crisis, Addiction & Recovery Infrastructure
Delaware County's overdose rate is 56% higher than the state average — and fentanyl has made the crisis deadlier.
🔥 Severe Local CrisisLocalStateFederal

Delaware County's opioid crisis is among the worst in Indiana by the numbers. The county recorded a fatal drug overdose rate of 41.6 per 100,000 residents — compared to 26.6 statewide — and ranked 4th worst in Indiana for fatal overdoses involving opioids. In 2019, Delaware County recorded 260 opioid prescriptions per 1,000 residents versus 200.4 statewide. Delaware County Jail alone had an estimated 252 prisoners in need of addiction treatment as of 2021. The City of Muncie's own Annual Action Plan documented that 65% of the homeless population is addicted to substances and 46% require mental health treatment. Fentanyl and synthetic opioids have accelerated the crisis. A weakened economy, high unemployment, and entrenched poverty are structural drivers. Indiana's $46M opioid abatement spending plan and the national settlement represent critical resources — but Medicaid cuts and federal behavioral health funding reductions threaten the treatment infrastructure that treatment-first strategies depend on.

3
Housing, Blight, Homelessness & Neighborhood Revitalization
Muncie is fighting back against decades of housing disinvestment — with a Land Bank, a READI 2.0 award, and a coalition committed to ending family homelessness.
📍 Active Investment UnderwayLocalStateFederal

Muncie's housing crisis has two faces: the ongoing damage of disinvestment — vacancy, blight, deteriorating stock — and a community fighting back with real tools. The Muncie Land Bank (MLB) has been operating since 2022, one of only four in Indiana, working to return vacant and blighted properties to productive use. In October 2025, Governor Braun and Mayor Ridenour announced a $6.75 million READI 2.0 award for Muncie housing — supporting 54 new downtown apartments plus 34 homes in the historic Old West End neighborhood, rooted in Ball State University urban planning research. The City's 2026 Annual Action Plan (CDBG/HOME) prioritizes affordable housing development, blight removal, home rehabilitation, down payment assistance, and permanent supportive housing for homeless individuals. Youth homelessness has surged post-COVID — YWCA Muncie reports 1 in 4 youth ages 14–24 have no stable place to live. The Delaware County Housing Consortium has set a bold goal: end family and youth homelessness in three years. That ambition needs state-level backup, not cuts.

Economic Revitalization, Workforce & Post-Industrial Recovery
Muncie lost its manufacturing identity decades ago. Healthcare and education now anchor the economy — but poverty, tariff uncertainty, and federal funding cuts threaten the recovery.
LocalStateFederal

Muncie Community Schools' enrollment peaked at 19,808 in 1967 — when manufacturing was at its apex. Today, 5,000 students remain. That demographic story mirrors the city's economic arc: factory jobs moved away, population fell, poverty deepened. Delaware County's 2025 Target Industry Study identified healthcare services and metals manufacturing as its two specialized growing clusters. The county's largest employment sectors are now healthcare and education — anchored by IU Health Ball Memorial Hospital and Ball State University. Local grantmakers invested $21.5 million in Muncie and Delaware County in recent years. A new $71M INOX Market Service headquarters and Balance Holdings' 33-acre I-69/SR-67 acquisition signal some investment momentum. But tariff volatility — the U.S. tariff rate hit 22.5% in 2025, the highest since 1909 — threatens the metals manufacturing cluster that Delaware County is counting on to grow. Federal workforce development funding cuts compound the challenge for a community still rebuilding its economic identity.

Public Schools, Education Funding & the MCS Transformation
Muncie Community Schools went through a nationally watched crisis and turnaround — and its fragile recovery is now vulnerable to federal funding cuts.
LocalStateFederal

Muncie Community Schools' story is one of the most unusual in American public education: after a financial crisis and state takeover, Ball State University became the governing authority — a national model. Under that partnership, enrollment stabilized around 5,000 students after decades of steep decline. Balanced budgets were achieved, $42 million was invested in capital improvements, teacher salaries were raised in the district's largest such action in its history, and new programs including Montessori, robotics, eSports, and career pathways were added. But this recovery is fragile. Federal education funding — the $1.8 billion Indiana receives annually — is under threat from Trump administration DEI compliance requirements. Muncie Community Schools serves a high-poverty, majority-minority student body that depends on Title I funding, special education support, and after-school programs. Statewide, Indiana's childcare voucher freeze and CCDF cuts squeeze working families in Delaware County — one of Indiana's highest-poverty communities. School meals funding cuts (Indiana opted out of SUN Bucks) hit MCS students especially hard.

Medicaid, Healthcare Access & the Safety Net Under Siege
22% of Muncie residents depend on Medicaid. Indiana's $68B contract rebid and H.R. 1 cuts threaten the system they rely on.
StateFederal

Muncie's healthcare economy is anchored by IU Health Ball Memorial Hospital — a major employer and critical community resource. But 22.1% of Muncie residents are on Medicaid, more than double the national average, making disruptions to the program an immediate community crisis. Indiana is simultaneously managing H.R. 1 Medicaid work requirements, a $68 billion managed care contract rebid (described by even Republican Rep. Ed Clere as "a recipe for disaster"), frozen disability waiver slots, and new immigration verification requirements that advocates say may be illegal. Federal and state budget pressure on Medicaid will fall hardest on Delaware County — where the population is older, poorer, and sicker than the state average. IU Health Ball Memorial, as the community's anchor hospital, is highly exposed to any reduction in Medicaid reimbursement. Muncie's recovery depends on healthcare jobs and services staying strong.

District & Race Context

What Makes HD-34 the Delaware County Polar Anchor

HD-34 is a defense seat — Sue Errington is retiring, and Sara Gullion must hold a +3.9D district against a Republican challenge. The Democratic advantage is real, but open-seat races always carry risk, especially in a midterm cycle where base turnout requires active cultivation. The challenge here is not persuasion — it is mobilizing a base that has reason to be angry and reason to show up.

Muncie is a community that has absorbed decades of hard news: factory closures, school crises, poverty, an opioid epidemic. But it is also a community that has demonstrated extraordinary resilience — the Ball State-MCS partnership is a national model, the Land Bank is doing real work, local philanthropy is investing $21.5 million, and a READI award just came through for 88 housing units. The Democratic frame in HD-34 is not despair — it is that government investment works, and Republicans in Washington are actively trying to cut it.

SNAP cuts, Medicaid disruption, education funding threats, federal behavioral health defunding — every one of these hits Delaware County harder than most Indiana communities, because the county's poverty rate, Medicaid dependency, and food insecurity are all well above state averages. The candidate who can connect those federal policy decisions to the daily lived experience of Muncie families — and who can demonstrate they will fight back — has a winning message.

The key voter in HD-34 is a Muncie resident who has seen their community struggle and is tired of being left behind. They may be skeptical about whether government works — but they're also directly affected by the programs being cut. They need a candidate who is local, credible, and clearly on their side. The institutions that anchor Muncie (Ball State, IU Health, Muncie Mission, YWCA) have deep community trust — a candidate who activates those networks has a strong ground game built in.
