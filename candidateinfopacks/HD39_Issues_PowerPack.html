Research Note

HD-39 is fundamentally different from other targets in the region. Hamilton County is Indiana's highest-income county, with college-educated, rapidly growing suburbs. Voters here care deeply about quality of life, schools, managed growth, democratic norms, and fiscal integrity — not just cost-of-living survival. Issues are sourced from local government reports, Indiana Citizen, WRTV, IBJ, and state policy analysis.

District Context

Harris won 46% of Hamilton County in 2024 — up from 37% for Clinton in 2016. Early voting in the 2026 primary surged past 2022 and 2024 levels. College-educated transplants from Indianapolis and other metros are reshaping the electorate. The redistricting fight at the Statehouse has further energized voters who feel their representation is being manipulated. This is persuadable suburban territory.

Managed Growth, Infrastructure & Traffic
The defining tension of Hamilton County: growth is happening faster than roads, schools, and services can keep up.
🔥 #1 Quality-of-Life Issue Local State

Hamilton County is among the fastest-growing counties in the United States, and its infrastructure is running years behind. Westfield grew from 21,000 people in 2008 to over 54,000 today — and roads built for a rural county are now major commuter arteries. INDOT's own data shows the U.S. 31/Meridian St. interchange — originally projected to handle 92,930 vehicles per day by 2035 — already exceeded 83,000 vehicles daily by 2023. Traffic fatalities and rear-end crashes have risen sharply on key corridors. Westfield unveiled an 8-year, $186 million infrastructure plan — with half of the 52 projects still unfunded. This is not an abstract policy issue; it affects residents' daily lives and safety.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Hamilton County's roads were designed for a rural county. They're not designed for what we've become — and the gap between our growth and our infrastructure is creating real safety hazards.
  • INDOT's own projections were wrong by more than a decade. Traffic on U.S. 31 hit 2035 levels in 2023. We can't keep planning for the past while residents sit in dangerous, gridlocked corridors.
  • Westfield has a $93 million hole in its infrastructure budget. Growth is good — but only if we build the plan to support it. Developers shouldn't profit while residents pay for the congestion they leave behind.
  • State investment in transportation infrastructure needs to keep pace with where Hoosiers actually live now — and Hamilton County is one of the fastest-growing places in the country.

School Overcrowding, Education Funding & Child Care
The fastest-growing school district in Indiana is scrambling — and so are working parents.
🔥 Active Local Conflict Local State Federal

Westfield Washington Schools — the fastest-growing school district in Indiana — is already redrawing attendance boundaries to manage overcrowding, with enrollment in K-4 elementary schools growing by 660 students in five years. In November 2025, a proposed school rebalancing plan triggered a parent revolt, with hundreds of families upset that children would be moved to a school adjacent to a major highway. Statewide, Indiana's childcare funding crisis is landing hard in Hamilton County too: state CCDF voucher enrollment has been frozen, and Indiana will issue no new child care vouchers through at least 2026. Federal education funding — including $107 million for Indiana programs — has been threatened by the Trump administration's DEI compliance requirements.

Property Taxes, Home Costs & SEA 1 Impact
Indiana's new property tax law benefits higher-value homes disproportionately — and threatens local government revenue.
State Local

Indiana's Senate Enrolled Act 1 (SEA 1), signed as a signature Braun initiative in 2025, restructures homestead property tax deductions through 2031. While marketed as relief for homeowners, analysis shows the reform disproportionately benefits those with higher home values — and the break-even point is $102,740 in assessed value. With an average Hamilton County home valued at $433,528, high-value homeowners gain more. Meanwhile, assessed values in Hamilton County rose well above the 12% statewide average in 2025 — and the property tax freeze in SEA 1 reduces revenue local governments can collect for roads, schools, and emergency services. This creates a structural tension: growing communities need more revenue as they grow, but the new law caps what can be collected.

Candidate Talking Points
  • The governor's property tax reform looks good on paper — but the fine print matters. In communities with modest home values, it actually makes things worse. It's tax relief for the wealthy disguised as help for everyone.
  • For Hamilton County specifically, the new law caps how much local governments can collect even as our population grows and demands for services increase. You can't fund a $186 million infrastructure backlog with a frozen levy.
  • Homeowners here have seen their assessed values surge 12% or more. That's real money — and people deserve a tax system that's transparent, fair, and actually funds the roads and schools they're paying for.
Sources: Hamilton County Democrats / HamcoDemsin, "Hoosier Property Tax Re-Re-Reform" (Dec. 2025); AribaTax, Indiana Assessed Values 2025 analysis (Apr. 2026); Indiana Fiscal Policy Institute SEA 1 analysis.

Mental Health, Youth Wellbeing & Behavioral Health Access
Affluent suburbs are not immune — Hamilton County has invested in crisis services, and that investment must continue.
Local State

Hamilton County made a visible commitment to behavioral health in recent years — including opening the Rely Center, the county's first dedicated 24/7 mental health crisis facility, operated by Aspire Indiana Health in Noblesville. The county website prominently features "NavigateHC," a behavioral health and wellness support system for residents. A former county judge publicly noted the connection between untreated mental health needs and early criminal involvement among youth. Teen mental health and youth vaping have also been flagged as public health priorities by the county. This is an area where Democrats can affirm local investment while pushing back on state-level defunding of mental health and Medicaid services that support these systems.

Candidate Talking Points

  • Mental health is not a "blue county" issue. Families in Noblesville, Westfield, and Carmel face anxiety, depression, teen substance use, and crisis — just like everywhere else. The question is whether their community is prepared to respond.
  • The state's Medicaid disruption — freezing disability waivers, rebidding $68 billion in managed care contracts — threatens the systems that behavioral health providers rely on. Local progress can be undone by Statehouse decisions.

Democratic Norms, Redistricting & Representation
Hamilton County voters showed up to fight gerrymandering — and they're not done.
📍 Local Voter Energy Governance State Federal

In November 2025, hundreds of Hamilton County residents — from both parties — turned out to a town hall in Fishers organized by ReCenter Indiana to voice concerns about Indiana's mid-decade congressional redistricting push. Attendees pushed back on the legislature prioritizing Trump's map-rigging request over actual constituent needs. The Indiana Senate ultimately voted 31-19 against the redrawn map, with 21 Republicans joining all 10 Democrats in opposition. That cross-partisan resistance is a signature Hamilton County political dynamic: college-educated voters who take democratic norms seriously, regardless of party. The redistricting controversy is energizing 2026 early voter turnout — already tracking above 2022 and 2024 levels in the primary.

Environment, Open Space & Quality of Life
At the Fishers redistricting town hall, a resident named the environment as their top concern. Hamilton County voters notice what's disappearing.
Local State

At the November 2025 redistricting town hall in Fishers, attendee James Todd said his top concern was "the environment and the destruction of our natural world here in Indiana" — a signal that environmental issues register for Hamilton County's college-educated, quality-of-life-focused electorate. Rapid development is converting farmland and natural areas at a fast pace; Westfield's growth alone has transformed thousands of acres. Residents who moved to the suburbs for space, trails, and a certain quality of life are watching it be built over. The Noblesville Parks Department has a five-year master plan under state review. Trail connectivity, green infrastructure, and stormwater management are live local issues alongside land use and density debates.

District & Race Context

What Makes HD-39 the Hamilton County Polar Anchor

HD-39 is Indiana's second "Polar Anchor" — the most competitive seat in a county that is undergoing rapid political realignment. Hamilton County is the wealthiest county in Indiana, home to Carmel, Fishers, Noblesville, and the booming suburb of Westfield. It has historically been deep red — but the Dem presidential share grew from 37% in 2016 to 46% in 2024, a nine-point swing driven by college-educated transplants, demographic change, and issues that cross party lines.

Lindsay Gramlich is challenging Republican Daniel Lopez in a seat that is statistically the most winnable for Democrats in Hamilton County. The surrounding legislative environment includes SD-29 (Kristina Moorhead, D+9 — one of the region's strongest Dem-performing senate seats) and SD-31 (Kerry Forestal, R+4.1, Hamilton and Marion counties). HD-39 is the gravitational center of this coalition: the most competitive race in a county trending sharply Democratic, with two contested Senate seats and a wave of House challengers operating in the same organizing geography. A strong performance by Lindsay Gramlich lifts every race in this pack.

The key voter in HD-39 is not a base Democrat. They're a college-educated homeowner, probably a transplant from another metro, who values good schools, functioning infrastructure, clean government, and fiscal responsibility. They may have voted Republican before, but they're increasingly uncomfortable. Issues that reinforce competence, quality of life, and democratic norms — rather than sharp ideological contrast — are most likely to move these voters.

The 2026 redistricting backlash, competitive primary environment, and surging early vote are all structural tailwinds. The race for Indiana's 5th Congressional District (CD-5) at the top of the ballot — with 7 Democrats competing, including J.D. Ford — also brings organizing infrastructure to the county that will benefit down-ballot candidates.
