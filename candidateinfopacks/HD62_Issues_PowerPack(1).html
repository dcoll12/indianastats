Rural Healthcare Access & the Hospital Desert Crisis
Brown County has no hospital. For its aging residents, getting care means driving — sometimes in emergencies.
🔥 Existential for Brown CountyStateFederal
Brown County: No Hospital Jackson Co: Schneck Medical Center Monroe Co: IU Health Bloomington

A November 2025 Brown County community health needs assessment — commissioned by local leaders and led by Indiana University — identified the county's aging population, lack of a hospital, and barriers to paying for care as its defining healthcare challenges. More than a quarter of residents are 65+, and there is no hospital. The IU team spotlighted community paramedicine as a promising solution: paramedics visiting elderly residents at home to manage chronic conditions before an emergency sends them to an ER miles away. Statewide, Indiana's 2025 Rural Health Report found that 25% of Indiana's 52 rural hospitals have already cut services and 15 have closed since 2010. EMS agencies in rural counties are understaffed and financially strained. Federal H.R. 1 Medicaid cuts and Indiana's $68B managed care contract rebid further threaten the rural health safety net.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Brown County has no hospital. For a county where one in four residents is 65 or older, that's not just an inconvenience — it's a public safety crisis. A heart attack doesn't wait for a 30-minute drive.
  • The community paramedicine model is exactly the kind of rural innovation we should be funding statewide: paramedics checking on elderly residents at home, managing chronic conditions before they become emergencies. Brown County is already exploring it. The Statehouse should be supporting it.
  • Indiana has lost 15 rural hospitals since 2010. Federal Medicaid cuts will accelerate closures. Communities like ours can't absorb another — and we need legislators who will fight to protect what remains.
  • The biggest healthcare concern Brown County residents cited wasn't finding a doctor. It was paying for care. That's a Medicaid and insurance access fight happening right now in Indianapolis and Washington, and it needs a champion from this district.

Substance Use, Recovery & Opioid Settlement Investment
Jackson County received $1.67M in opioid settlement funds — and the debate over how to spend it is still live.
📍 Active in Jackson CountyLocalState
Jackson County: Primary Issue Brown County: Rural isolation risk factors

Jackson County has built dedicated substance use infrastructure — alcohol and drug courts, a community coalition, Schneck Medical Center partnerships — and is now managing the distribution of $1,666,863 from the national opioid settlement. County commissioners have debated the use of funds, with Commissioner Markel stating: "I want the main focus to be treatment." Jackson County will receive $82,076 annually through 2038 — ongoing accountability funding. The Seymour Tribune published a 2026 special section on the county's opioid situation, signaling this is still high-visibility for local voters. Indiana's 2025 drug prevention plan identifies Jackson County specifically, with THC and other substances joining opioids as current concerns.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Jackson County is getting over $1.6 million in opioid settlement funds, and the community has clearly said: put it toward treatment. Recovery coaches, transitional housing, treatment programs — those investments save lives and rebuild families. We shouldn't let that money get diluted.
  • Jackson County already has the infrastructure — drug courts, a coalition, Schneck Medical. What it needs is sustained political will and a legislator who shows up for the recovery community, not just at election time.
  • The opioid crisis didn't happen to Jackson County because of bad choices. It happened because of predatory pharmaceutical companies. The settlement money is accountability — let's use it to heal.

Housing Affordability & Workforce Cost Burden
Bloomington's housing costs are squeezing workers out; Brown County tourism is squeezing year-round residents out.
StateLocal
Monroe County: Wage-housing squeeze Brown County: Tourism/rental pressure Jackson County: Workforce housing gap

Monroe County's housing crisis sharpened in 2025. The IU Business Research Center found Bloomington metro workers earning 11.9% less than the average Hoosier while living in a market where homes list 18.1% above the state average. By August 2025, an average Bloomington worker had to work 34% more days of their life than the average Hoosier to afford a home — up from 21% just one year earlier. IU graduates leave because they can't afford to stay. Brown County faces a parallel but different squeeze: tourism and second-home demand is driving up values while limited buildable land (most is federally or state-owned) constrains supply, pricing out year-round residents at modest incomes. Cook Medical in Jackson County began building homes for its own employees — a sign of how acute the workforce housing shortage has become in the region.

Candidate Talking Points
  • In Bloomington, workers earn less than the state average and pay more for a home than almost anywhere else in Indiana. Young people build careers there and leave because they can't afford to stay. That's an economic drain on the whole region.
  • Brown County's character — its forests, its art community, its small-town feel — is exactly what draws visitors. But when short-term rental demand prices out the people who actually live there year-round, we lose the community that makes the county worth visiting in the first place.
  • Cook Medical in Jackson County started building homes for their own employees. That's what happens when state policy fails — employers become housing developers. We need real policy solutions, not corporate workarounds.
  • Rural Indiana's housing problem isn't the same as the cities'. It needs rural-specific solutions: land bank tools, affordable housing trust funds that work in low-density markets, and policies that protect year-round residents from being outbid by second-home buyers.

Rural Economic Vitality, Tourism & Broadband
Brown County's economy runs on tourism and remote workers. Jackson County runs on manufacturing. Both need connectivity and economic diversification to thrive.
LocalStateFederal
Brown County: Tourism/remote work economy Jackson County: Manufacturing/I-65 corridor Monroe County: IU entrepreneurship growth

Brown County is a textbook rural tourism economy — approximately 40% of its GDP comes from tourism, most land is federally or state-owned (limiting the tax base), and school enrollment has been declining. Its economic future depends on attracting remote workers and retirees — which requires reliable broadband. Fiber technically reaches 90% of Brown County, but adoption and affordability gaps remain. The Indiana Rural Health Association's 2025 report flagged broadband as a critical barrier to telehealth, education, and remote work across rural Indiana. Jackson County's manufacturing base is exposed to tariff volatility — the 2025 U.S. effective tariff rate hit 22.5%, the highest since 1909 — creating real uncertainty for employers along the I-65 corridor. Bloomington's entrepreneurship scene is a bright spot (private business up 4.6% in 2025) but the housing barrier is preventing talent retention.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Brown County's economy depends on visitors — and visitors depend on the community being authentic and alive year-round. Declining school enrollment and rising housing costs are the slow erosion of exactly what makes this place worth coming to.
  • Broadband is rural infrastructure, the same as roads and water lines. Telehealth, remote work, small business — all of it runs on connectivity. The state has committed resources; we need to make sure Brown County sees real results, not just coverage maps.
  • Jackson County manufacturers are caught in tariff whiplash they didn't create. When Washington changes trade policy overnight, Seymour workers pay the price. We need state-level economic resilience planning and support — not just hoping the trade war settles down.
  • Bloomington's entrepreneurship scene is genuinely growing. We should be building the regional housing and infrastructure policy that lets those businesses — and their employees — put down roots in communities across Brown, Jackson, and Monroe Counties.

Aging Population, Senior Services & Long-Term Care
Brown County's electorate is older than the state average — and older rural voters experience healthcare, transportation, and financial isolation most acutely.
LocalState
Brown County: Core Issue — 25%+ 65+ Jackson County: Rural senior access gaps

Brown County's health needs survey found the lowest self-rated scores in financial and social health — especially among seniors and those with limited mobility. The survey skewed heavily female and 60+, reflecting the actual population. The IU assessment noted fewer multigenerational households and growing social isolation as compounding risk factors. There is no public transit in rural Brown County, leaving seniors completely dependent on others for appointments outside the county. Indiana's Medicaid disruptions — disability waiver freezes, Pathways for Aging managed care disruption — directly harm this population. Fifteen rural hospitals have closed in Indiana since 2010. The combination of no local hospital, no transit, and shrinking state services creates a layered crisis for Brown County's most vulnerable residents.

Schools, Childcare & Rural Education Investment
Declining enrollment in Brown County and statewide childcare cuts are squeezing rural schools and working families from both ends.
StateFederalLocal
Brown County: Systemic enrollment decline Jackson County: 4 K-12 public systems Monroe County: IU/higher ed stress

Brown County's school enrollment has been declining systemically — a structural warning sign for any rural community. Statewide, Indiana's childcare crisis hits rural areas especially hard: CCDF voucher enrollment is frozen through 2026, On My Way Pre-K was cut by more than half, and no new vouchers will be issued until at least 2027. In rural communities with limited providers, the voucher freeze often means parents leave the workforce entirely. In Monroe County, IU faced economic stress in 2025 — wages fell, employment shrank — with downstream effects on K-12 pipelines and community programs across the region. A threatened $107 million in federal education funding under Trump administration DEI compliance requirements affects rural districts too: after-school programs, reading intervention, special services. Sprunica Elementary in Brown County has been winning statewide recognition for student innovation — that kind of success needs stable funding.

District & Race Context

What Makes HD-62 the Southern Rural Polar Anchor

HD-62 is the most Dem-favorable of the three Polar Anchors at +1.4D — meaning Amy Huffman Oliver is running in terrain that is statistically tilted her way, with a clear path to victory if organized. Dave Hall (R) holds the seat from Nashville, the Brown County seat. The three-county district has genuinely distinct identities: Brown is rural/retirement/tourism; Jackson is manufacturing/Seymour/recovery; Monroe (portion) is Bloomington-adjacent and university-influenced.

The unifying frame across all three counties is rural communities being left behind by a Statehouse focused on suburban growth. Healthcare deserts in Brown County, opioid recovery funding debates in Jackson, the housing-wage squeeze in Monroe — residents across this district feel the gap between what state government delivers to Hamilton County and what reaches them.

SD-40, covering Monroe County and held by Democrat Shelli Yoder at a +29.2D performance margin, demonstrates that strong Democratic infrastructure exists in the Monroe County portion of the district. That organizing base is a real structural asset for Huffman Oliver. The challenge is persuading Brown and Jackson County voters who may be culturally Republican but economically aligned with Democratic policies on healthcare, rural investment, and recovery.

The key voter in HD-62 is a rural resident — possibly a retiree in Nashville who can't get to a doctor, a manufacturing worker in Seymour watching trade policy whipsaw their employer, or a Bloomington-adjacent renter who can't afford to buy. A candidate who shows up, speaks plainly about economic dignity and healthcare access, and has genuine local roots can win here. The issues are real, the index favors Democrats, and the Bloomington base provides organizing infrastructure.
