Cummins, Durable Goods Manufacturing & Tariff Headwinds
Bartholomew County's entire economic identity is built around Cummins and durable goods manufacturing — exactly the sector IU economists say will lag in 2026 due to tariff-driven uncertainty.
🔥 Core Bartholomew Issue Federal Local
Bartholomew: Cummins as dominant employer; top 1% mfg. nationally Johnson: Malarkey Roofing $200M facility; tariff input cost exposure

Bartholomew County's economy is extraordinary in its manufacturing concentration — ranked in the top 1% of U.S. counties, anchored for over a century by Cummins Inc. (founded in Columbus, 1919). Cummins produces diesel and natural gas engines, power generators, and components — durable goods squarely in the crosshairs of the 2025-2026 economic headwinds. IU Columbus economists at the annual Futurecast panel in November 2025 specifically warned that "with Bartholomew County's heavy reliance on building durable goods, the community will likely lag behind the rest of the state next year." The 2026 forecast for Columbus GDP: flat to 0-2% growth, contingent on "continued interest rate reductions, increased revenues for Cummins, additional capital expenditures toward A.I." Even a modest Cummins revenue shortfall ripples through the entire Columbus economy — supplier businesses, retail, restaurants, and local government revenue all track Cummins. The one bright spot: Cummins has benefited from strong demand for power generators from data centers training AI systems, which partially offset tariff pressure in Q2 2025. But tariff chaos with nearly 400 adjustments in 2025 continues to create planning uncertainty. For Johnson County, the $200 million Malarkey Roofing facility (opening 2026 in Franklin) brings manufacturing jobs — but roofing materials and construction inputs are directly affected by tariff-driven cost increases in raw materials.

Johnson County: Growth Without Investment — Infrastructure Falling Behind
Johnson County is booming — but IU economists say it's growing "below its potential." Bargersville is managing 873-acre "generational" developments while trying to keep infrastructure ahead of growth.
📍 Core Johnson Issue Local State
Johnson: Bargersville, Greenwood, Whiteland, Franklin — all actively managing growth surge Bartholomew: Columbus housing inventory still far below pre-pandemic levels

Johnson County is among Indiana's fastest-growing counties — and the growth story is real: a $59 million mixed-use development (The Jefferson) in Bargersville, an 873-acre Pulte Iron Horse residential development named a "generational project," 1,625 single-family homes and 594 apartment units under construction in Greenwood, and a Franklin College Innovation Park planned for 250 acres. But the IU Kelley economists told Johnson County business leaders directly in November 2025: the county will grow "below its potential" in 2026 because national uncertainty and tariff-driven durable goods weakness are restraining the investment that would support it. Bargersville's town manager explicitly said: "We cannot get this wrong" — and that he wants infrastructure ahead of development, not paid for on the backs of current taxpayers. This tension — rapid growth, limited infrastructure investment, economic uncertainty restraining tax revenue — is the core challenge for Johnson County communities. HD-47's Bargersville is at the epicenter. The Whiteland tornado recovery (March 2025 EF-2/EF-3) added another dimension of community resilience and infrastructure demand. In Columbus (Bartholomew County), housing inventory reached its highest point since COVID in early 2025 — but still at just 146 units, vs. 423–456 pre-pandemic. The housing supply shortage remains a drag on economic growth according to IU economists.

School Funding Squeeze & Local Government Budget Cuts
SEA 1's $744M statewide school funding cut affects every school district in both counties — while local governments produce formal analyses showing they'll need to offset losses with wheel taxes or new local income taxes.
State Local
Bartholomew: Formal county SEA 1 analysis; three school districts affected; wheel tax being studied Johnson County: All districts exposed; rapidly growing schools hit hardest by funding cap

Both counties produced formal analyses of SEA 1's impact on local government budgets and school funding. In Bartholomew County, a detailed legislative overview presentation was prepared examining SEA 1 impacts on the Bartholomew Consolidated School Corporation, Flat Rock-Hawcreek School Corp., Edinburgh Community School Corp., and county government — and specifically noting that a countywide surtax/wheel tax generating up to $4.99 million annually was being studied to "offset some of the losses due to SEA 1." The statewide $744.4 million in school property tax revenue losses projected by the Legislative Services Agency hit all 160+ school corporations — but fast-growing school districts like those in Johnson County face a compounding squeeze: enrollment grows, but the funding formula can't keep up with what SEA 1 took away. Johnson County schools serve a rapidly growing student population in communities like Bargersville, Whiteland, and Greenwood — all of which are receiving thousands of new residents and hundreds of new students annually. The new SEA 1 restriction on charter school revenue sharing (starting 2028) adds another layer of financial complexity for traditional public schools. The full impact of SEA 1 continues phasing in through 2031.

Healthcare Access, Medicaid & Opioid Recovery Investment
H.R. 1's $23B Indiana Medicaid cut threatens coverage for working families across both counties — while Johnson County's active opioid settlement investment program is exactly what's at risk when federal and state funding shrinks.
State Federal
Johnson County: Active opioid settlement fund; BLOOM family program; Tara Treatment expansion Bartholomew: Bartholomew County Health Department; Medicaid-dependent working families

H.R. 1 (signed July 4, 2025) is projected by KFF to cut Indiana's Medicaid funding by 15% — approximately $23 billion over 2027–2036 — the largest Medicaid cut in program history. Indiana's two U.S. senators voted for it. This hits Bartholomew and Johnson county families across the income spectrum: manufacturing and service workers who depend on the Healthy Indiana Plan (HIP), people with disabilities awaiting home-based services, and children in Hoosier Healthwise. Johnson County has specifically invested opioid settlement funds in meaningful local recovery infrastructure — in 2026, funding went to BLOOM (a family treatment program for opioid-exposed infants), Tara Treatment Center (expanding outpatient services), and other evidence-based programs. That infrastructure is built on federal and state funding flows that H.R. 1 puts at risk. Medicaid is the primary payer for substance use treatment and behavioral health services. New HIP work requirements (July 1, 2026) add eligibility risk for some of the most vulnerable workers. Indiana's Medicaid managed care rebid left 300,000 Hoosiers scrambling to change insurers in under 60 days after MDwise was removed in November 2025. Disability waiver slots partially reopened in July 2025 but still cover only one-third of the 13,000 on the waiting list.

Wages, Child Care & Working Family Investment
Indiana's childcare voucher freeze and Pre-K cuts hit working families in Columbus and Johnson County's booming communities — while wages have lagged in real terms even in manufacturing-heavy Bartholomew County.
State Federal
Bartholomew: Manufacturing wages; childcare for shift workers Johnson: Boom of young families needs childcare infrastructure

Indiana's statewide childcare crisis reaches directly into Bartholomew and Johnson counties. State CCDF reimbursement rates for school-age childcare were cut up to 35% in fall 2025. New childcare vouchers were frozen — no new low-income families added through at least 2026, with 35,000+ on the statewide waitlist. On My Way Pre-K was cut by more than half statewide. For families in Columbus's manufacturing economy — where shift work is common and both parents often work — affordable, accessible childcare isn't optional. In Johnson County, where thousands of young families are moving in each year, childcare availability and affordability is a growth constraint: the communities can build the houses but can't support the families if childcare doesn't keep pace. Indiana ranked 36th nationally in per capita income in 2024. Columbus manufacturing wages, while among the state's stronger performers, still operate in an environment where real wage gains have been modest over the past decade when adjusted for inflation. Federal proposals to cut HUD funding by nearly half would further squeeze the affordable housing infrastructure both counties need to serve lower-income working families.

SD-43, HD-73, and rural Bartholomew County communities face the classic rural squeeze: aging population, limited healthcare access, declining services, and a Statehouse focused on suburban growth. Every door knocked builds the future.

Bartholomew: Rural townships; Edinburgh; Flat Rock-Hawcreek school district Johnson: Greensburg area (HD-73); rural southern Johnson County

Indiana's broadband maps show persistent rural connectivity gaps. Every candidate who runs in these districts, every volunteer who knocks a door, and every issue conversation that happens builds the organizational infrastructure and political credibility that makes future cycles more competitive. The issues — healthcare, wages, school funding, opioid recovery, economic uncertainty — are real and universal. The difference between pioneer territory and competitive territory is organizing, over time.

Turnout Pack Strategy

🌱 What Pioneer District Work Actually Accomplishes

  • Voter file building: Every door knock updates contact information, identifies Democratic-leaning households, and produces data that makes the next cycle more efficient. In pioneer districts, the voter file is often years out of date — working it is itself a victory.
  • Volunteer training: Campaigns in pioneer districts train the next generation of canvassers, phone bankers, and organizers who will staff competitive races in adjacent cycles. The skills don't disappear when the campaign ends.
  • Margin movement: Even a 5-point improvement in a +34R district reduces the Republican margin of victory, costs them resources they'd rather spend elsewhere, and signals that no district is "safe." Forcing Republicans to defend home turf changes the statewide map.
  • Issue penetration: Talking about real things — Cummins and tariffs, school funding, childcare, Medicaid — in communities that haven't heard a Democratic candidate in years plants seeds. Issues stick even when candidates don't win. The next candidate benefits from every conversation this one has.
  • Infrastructure for 2028 and beyond: SD-41, HD-47, and HD-73 are all in the territory where demographic change (suburban growth in Johnson County, manufacturing workforce shifts in Bartholomew) could eventually move the competitiveness index. The organizing infrastructure built in 2026 positions future candidates to exploit that movement.

The Four Candidates of This Pack — Why They Run and What They Build

Ross Thomas (SD-41, +34.4R, Columbus) is the Polar Anchor — the most reachable seat in the pack. SD-41 covers Bartholomew and Johnson counties, and the issues are genuinely real: Cummins and tariff-driven economic uncertainty, SEA 1's school funding cuts, housing inventory below pre-pandemic norms, and childcare gaps for manufacturing families. Columbus has a self-aware civic culture — the city invested in world-class architecture, Cummins has a history of community investment, and there's a tradition of civic engagement that a credible Democratic candidate can tap. Running hard in Columbus specifically, where Democratic voters are more concentrated, can produce real vote totals even in a difficult year.

Michael Potter (HD-47, +44.1R, Bargersville) is running in Johnson County's fastest-growing community — a place where the issues of growth without adequate infrastructure, school funding pressure, childcare gaps for young families, and economic uncertainty about the future are live and real. The district may be R+44 overall, but Bargersville's explosive growth is bringing demographic diversity, younger voters, and residents who haven't formed deep partisan attachments yet. This is exactly the kind of district where showing up, talking about real things, and building relationships can produce surprising results over multiple cycles.

Allen Miller (HD-73, +49.6R, Greensburg) runs in Decatur and southern Johnson County terrain — deep rural, but with real economic concerns. Greensburg's manufacturing base (including Honda supplier relationships) faces the same tariff uncertainty as Columbus. Rural healthcare access, Medicaid stability, and school funding are live issues in communities that haven't seen competitive races. Every vote total above the party baseline is a signal, and every volunteer trained is infrastructure.

Byron Holland (SD-43, +53.1R, Austin) runs in some of Indiana's toughest political terrain. Austin is Scott County — rural, historically Democratic in generations past, now deeply Republican. Running here is pure pioneer work: building the voter file, having conversations that haven't happened in years, and demonstrating that Democrats are willing to show up everywhere. The issues are real — rural healthcare deserts, opioid recovery, economic despair in communities left behind by state development priorities. The return is organizational, not electoral, in this cycle.

Taken together, these four candidates represent a coordinated presence across a large swath of central and south-central Indiana. SD-41 is the investment with the highest near-term return. SD-43 and HD-73 are pure infrastructure. HD-47 is the most interesting long-term opportunity given Johnson County's demographics. All four build something that lasts beyond election day.
