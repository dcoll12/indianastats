Local Government Budget Squeeze & Public Safety
SEA 1 cut $25M from Fort Wayne city revenue and $11M from Fort Wayne Community Schools — Fort Wayne's Democratic mayor testified against it at the Statehouse.
🔥 Critical Local Issue State Local Both Packs
Allen County: $25M city cut; $11M FWCS school cut DeKalb: All school districts engaged legislators on SB 1

Gov. Braun's Senate Enrolled Act 1 (2025) is delivering documented, concrete budget damage to Fort Wayne and Allen County. Fort Wayne Mayor Sharon Tucker — a Democrat — testified before the Statehouse House Ways and Means Committee in March 2025 about the harm to her city's budget and then created an "SB1 Fiscal Task Force" to manage the multi-year fallout. The bottom line she presented: Fort Wayne will receive $25 million less in property tax revenue due to property tax caps, with further losses expected as later elements of SEA 1 phase in through 2031. "About half of our general fund revenue comes from property taxes," Tucker said. "Between 50 to 60 percent is for public safety. Our police officers, our firefighters. There would be some tough decisions that would have to be made." Fort Wayne Community Schools — Indiana's largest school district by enrollment — faces an $10.7–$11 million annual property tax shortfall from SEA 1, following a $5 million cut the previous year that already forced the elimination of ~20 central office positions. All four Allen County school districts (FWCS, NACS, SACS, EACS) publicly engaged their state legislators about the harm. The same pattern plays out in DeKalb County, where all three school districts were tracking SB 1 impacts. Northeast Indiana mayors gathered in November 2025 to collectively warn residents to "get used to fewer services."

Housing Affordability — A Decade of Price Surge
Allen County home prices are up 115% since 2015. Wages haven't kept pace in real terms. Rent crossed $1,000/month for the first time in 2024. First-time buyers are squeezed.
🔥 Documented — PFW Research Local State Federal Both Packs
Allen County: Median home $258K — up 115% since 2015 DeKalb: Auburn named 10th most affordable U.S. city — but rural housing stock aging

Purdue University Fort Wayne's Community Research Institute has tracked Allen County's housing market comprehensively. The findings are stark: the median home sales price reached $258,661 in mid-2025, up from $120,000 in 2015 — a 115% increase in unadjusted terms, or 64% when adjusted for inflation. Either way, it far outpaces the 32% rise in the Consumer Price Index. Allen County rent crossed the $1,000/month threshold for the first time in 2024, having jumped $90 between 2019 and 2021 and another $111 between 2021 and 2022. Wages, when adjusted for inflation, peaked in 2021 and then declined in 2022 and 2023, only partially recovering in 2024. The result: first-time homebuyers face a market where prices have doubled while their real wages have not. The rental vacancy rate in Fort Wayne is 6% — below average — which keeps competition for units high and prices elevated. Fort Wayne has 47 affordable housing properties, with Section 8 vouchers covering a maximum of $980–$1,198 for a two-bedroom — barely enough in a rising market. Federal proposals to cut HUD funding nearly in half would further diminish what little affordable housing infrastructure exists. Allen County is growing — it added 3,235 residents in the past year alone — which adds demand without necessarily adding affordable supply.

Manufacturing Economy & Tariff Uncertainty
Fort Wayne's nearly 38,000 manufacturing jobs — heavy in auto supply chain and logistics — are directly in the crosshairs of 2025–2026 tariff chaos and consumer slowdown.
Federal State Both Packs
Allen County: 37,829 manufacturing jobs; GM supplier network; Amazon logistics DeKalb: Continental, Walmart DC; manufacturing pipeline

The Fort Wayne MSA's economy rests substantially on manufacturing and logistics. As of March 2025, the metro had 37,829 manufacturing jobs — a cornerstone of working-class employment in Allen County. The PFW 2026 forecast explicitly warned that this base is vulnerable: "An economic downturn could negatively affect the Fort Wayne metro's nearly 38,000 manufacturing jobs. A spending slowdown creates a double whammy for manufacturing because consumers pull back on spending for durable goods, such as vehicles, and the wage premium manufacturing employees enjoy dissipates with layoffs or elimination of overtime." Fort Wayne is heavily embedded in the auto supply chain — General Motors, its suppliers, and adjacent manufacturers form a significant cluster. The 2025 tariffs on Canada and Mexico specifically targeted the North American automotive supply chain; automakers are "rearranging supply chains and production decisions" according to Indiana's own economic forecast. Three Amazon warehouses in Fort Wayne add logistics dependency — a sector sensitive to consumer spending slowdowns. DeKalb County's Continental tech center consolidation in Auburn adds a white-collar manufacturing technology dimension. Statewide, nearly 400 tariff adjustments in 2025 alone created business planning chaos, and Ball State's CBER projected "mild stagflation" — slower growth with elevated prices — as the base case for 2026. Indiana's own forecast projected unemployment rising from 3.6% to 4.4% statewide.

Wages, Inflation & the Cost-of-Living Squeeze
Allen County wages lag behind the state and nation in inflation-adjusted terms. Residents are "upset by higher prices on basic needs" according to PFW's own economic analysis heading into 2026.
State Federal Both Packs
Allen County: Wages lagged nation & state for decades; real peak was 2021 DeKalb: Rural wages; commuter economy to Fort Wayne

Purdue Fort Wayne's 2026 economic forecast opens with a striking observation: "Heading into 2026, Fort Wayne-area residents are upset by higher prices on basic needs. Their frustration lines up with the data." Allen County's average private sector wage has lagged behind the nation and state for decades. When adjusted for inflation, wages peaked in 2021 at $61,737, declined in 2022 and 2023, then partially recovered in 2024 — but the 2024 average still lags the adjusted wage of 2020 and 2021. Indiana overall ranked 36th nationally in per capita real income in 2024, consistently at the bottom among Midwestern peers. The squeeze is multidimensional: groceries, rent, and home prices have all risen faster than wages for most of the past decade. PFW's analysis notes that while Allen County's unadjusted wages appear to have risen 41.8% since 2015, in inflation-adjusted terms the gain is only 10.1% — a crucial distinction. Food costs doubled between 2020 and 2022 (in unadjusted dollars) and have flatlined at those high levels since. Workers in Fort Wayne are making more nominal dollars but buying less. That mismatch between economic optimism in headline statistics and daily cost-of-living strain is the political reality of this district.

Healthcare Access & Medicaid Stability
H.R. 1's $23B in Indiana Medicaid cuts threatens Parkview and Lutheran Health — Fort Wayne's two dominant healthcare employers — and eliminates coverage for thousands of Allen and DeKalb County residents.
State Federal Both Packs
Allen County: Parkview Health, Lutheran Health — major employers dependent on Medicaid reimbursement DeKalb: Rural access; limited providers; Medicaid essential

Fort Wayne's healthcare sector is one of its largest employers — anchored by Parkview Health and Lutheran Health, both of which receive significant Medicaid reimbursement. H.R. 1, signed July 4, 2025, is projected by KFF to cut Indiana's federal Medicaid funding by 15%, or approximately $23 billion over 2027–2036. This is the largest Medicaid cut in the history of the program. Indiana's Sens. Young and Banks both voted for it. Simultaneously, the state is rebidding $68 billion in Medicaid managed care contracts (the "Mother of All Procurements"), with 300,000 Hoosiers given less than 60 days to change insurers after MDwise was removed from the program in November 2025. New HIP work requirements take effect July 1, 2026, adding more coverage risk. Disability waivers — partially reopened July 2025 — still cover only one-third of the 13,000 people on the waitlist for home-and-community-based services. For DeKalb County's rural communities, Medicaid is often the only affordable pathway to healthcare access given limited provider options and distance to major medical centers.

Child Care Crisis & School Funding Stability
Indiana froze childcare vouchers and cut reimbursement rates 35% while simultaneously stripping $11M from FWCS — a double squeeze on Allen County's working families from both ends.
State Federal Local Both Packs
Allen County: FWCS $11M school hit; childcare desert in some neighborhoods DeKalb: Rural districts; fewer providers; school construction underway despite budget pressure

Indiana's childcare crisis intersects directly with Fort Wayne's cost-of-living squeeze. State CCDF reimbursement rates were cut up to 35% for school-age childcare in fall 2025. New low-income vouchers were frozen — no new families added through 2026, with more than 35,000 statewide on the waitlist. On My Way Pre-K was cut by more than half statewide. In a city where manufacturing and healthcare workers often work non-standard shifts, access to reliable and affordable childcare is essential to workforce participation. Without it, parents — disproportionately women and single parents — drop out of the labor force, reducing household income and increasing economic vulnerability. Meanwhile, Fort Wayne Community Schools faces an $11 million annual shortfall from SEA 1 on top of a previous $5 million cut that forced position eliminations. All four Allen County school districts are in active dialogue with legislators about impacts. In DeKalb County, all three school districts (DeKalb Eastern, DeKalb Central, Garrett-Keyser-Butler) are proceeding with capital improvements — but under financial pressure that the property tax reform exacerbates. School construction in progress signals community commitment; state funding policy working against it signals Statehouse disconnect.

DeKalb County: Rural Investment, Broadband & Population Decline
SD-14 spans Allen and DeKalb — two different worlds. DeKalb saw record private investment in 2024 but lost population year-over-year. Rural broadband and sustained investment are essential to keeping it competitive.
📍 SD-14 Specific Local State Federal
DeKalb: Population declined 2024–2025; $760M investment record but rural gaps persist

DeKalb County presents a paradox: it recorded a record $760 million in private investment in 2024 — named a top-10 U.S. Micropolitan by Site Selection Magazine four of the past five years — yet it was one of 26 Indiana counties that lost population between 2024 and 2025. The county is actively working to address this: READI funding secured for two projects (The Auburn mixed-use development and the Bohn Redevelopment in Butler), an EPA brownfield assessment grant, new housing construction in all three cities, and agricultural expansions at multiple farms. Rural broadband ("rural connectivity") remains explicitly identified as a top priority, with Surf Internet and other providers making progress on fiber — essential both for quality of life and for attracting employers. The Continental tech center consolidation in Auburn brought white-collar jobs. The Walmart distribution center expansion in Garrett added logistics employment. But population decline is a warning signal: if people can't afford to stay, or don't find services adequate, the investment story stalls. Federal cuts to HUD, USDA rural programs, and Medicaid compound the challenge of sustaining rural community viability.

Why These Packs Are Combined — And How They Work Together

HD-81 and SD-14 share the same core geography (Allen County, Fort Wayne), the same economic story (manufacturing, housing costs, SEA 1 budget impacts, wages lagging prices), and the same strategic purpose: contest hard in R+24 and R+28 terrain to force Republican spending, demonstrate Democratic viability in northeast Indiana, and lift SD-19's Timothy Murphy in the wider regional wave. Combining them into one research document means candidates and organizers have a unified, referenced issue base rather than duplicated work — with the DeKalb-specific section giving Blaine Sefton additional material tailored to his district's rural geography.

HD-81 (Sharon Wight, +24.2R) is the nearer seat. Allen County is growing, has a Democratic mayor who has publicly and vocally pushed back against state Statehouse overreach on property taxes, and has a manufacturing/working-class base that has tangible grievances around wages, housing costs, school funding, and childcare. The issues are documented, local, and concrete. The district covers the western portion of Fort Wayne and Allen County where these economic realities land hardest on working families.

SD-14 (Blaine Sefton, +28.3R) spans both Allen and DeKalb counties. Fort Wayne anchors the population, but DeKalb's rural community — with its paradox of strong investment and persistent population loss — is where the candidate can demonstrate a different Democratic vision: one that shows up for rural communities, fights for broadband, and connects federal and state policy failures to everyday rural life. Incumbent Dr. Tyler Johnson is a relatively new senator; a credible, well-resourced challenger running on concrete local issues forces real political investment.

SD-19 (Timothy Murphy, +39.5R, Travis Holdman incumbent) benefits from the organizing activity and Democratic presence created by the other two races. Holdman is the author of Senate Bill 1 — the same law that cut $25M from Fort Wayne's budget, $11M from Fort Wayne Community Schools, and created budget freezes across northeast Indiana. That's a direct, documented, local connection between the SD-19 race and the issues every other candidate in this pack is running on. Murphy doesn't need to win to matter — he needs to run hard, make Holdman spend, and put the author of SEA 1 on defense in his home region.
