Priority Issues — Hendricks County / HD-40
1
Public School Funding & the Referendum Fight
🔥 Hot Local Issue Local State

In November 2025, Avon Community School Corporation — with roughly 11,000 students and a heavily residential tax base — won a hard-fought referendum renewal battle that exposed a deep fault line in Hendricks County politics. Lt. Gov. Micah Beckwith personally door-knocked in Avon urging a "no" vote against the district's operating levy renewal. Avon voters rejected him: 65% voted yes. The referendum funds 170 teacher and staff positions, smaller class sizes, and competitive pay. Meanwhile, Gov. Mike Braun's property tax reform package (SEA 1, 2025) is projected to strip $744.4 million in total school property tax revenue statewide through 2028 — with Avon Community School Corp. especially vulnerable because its tax base is overwhelmingly residential (capped at the lowest rate). School Superintendent Scott Wyndham explicitly warned that Avon faces "the most exposure" to the new law. A new state mandate also requires districts to share local property tax revenue with charter schools starting in 2028.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Avon voters spoke clearly in November: 65% said yes to their schools when the Lt. Governor flew in to say no. Republicans at the Statehouse aren't listening to Hendricks County families — they're undermining them.
  • Gov. Braun's property tax reform will strip hundreds of millions from Indiana school districts over three years. Avon Community Schools — because its tax base is heavily residential — is among the most exposed in the state. That's a direct threat to class sizes, teacher pay, and the programs that make Avon schools worth living here for.

  • Our kids deserve fully staffed classrooms and teachers who can afford to stay. That requires a Statehouse that treats public education as the investment it is, not a line item to squeeze.

2
Rapid Growth, Traffic Congestion & Infrastructure
📍 Local Context Local State

Hendricks County is among Indiana's fastest-growing counties, driven by Indianapolis suburban expansion, logistics industry growth (the Ronald Reagan Parkway corridor is a major warehousing and distribution hub), and residential development in Avon, Brownsburg, and Plainfield. The county is actively extending the Ronald Reagan Parkway — Phase 1A opened to CR 750N in November 2025 at a cost of nearly $38 million, with Phase 1B (to CR 1000N, approaching the Boone County line) beginning construction in winter 2025–26. Despite this investment, commuter congestion, freight truck traffic, road capacity, and neighborhood-level road safety remain daily concerns for residents in HD-40. The county's Plan Commission completed a 2025 Comprehensive Plan to guide growth. Residents are navigating a tension between welcoming economic opportunity and protecting quality of life, road safety, and community character in their fast-changing neighborhoods.

3
Housing Affordability & Attainability
Local State Federal

Hendricks County's rapid growth has come with rising housing costs. Estimated median home values in the county reached $338,100 in 2024 — more than double the $132,100 recorded in 2000. The median monthly housing cost for homeowners with a mortgage now runs $1,832 per month. Avon has the highest property tax rate in the county at 1.13%, and with SEA 1 of 2025 reshaping how tax caps interact with assessed values, homeowners face continued uncertainty about their bills. Affordable rental housing is critically scarce: the county has only 288 income-based apartments for a population approaching 200,000, and rental vacancy rates are below average, pushing rents upward. Section 8 vouchers cover just a fraction of what's needed, and federal proposals to cut HUD funding by nearly half would further reduce affordable housing availability. First-time buyers — especially working families — are increasingly priced out of a market that was built on the premise of Indy-area affordability.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Hendricks County used to be a place where working families could afford to buy a home. A median home value over $338,000 is changing that — and we need state policies that support attainable housing, not just luxury development.
  • Young families, essential workers, teachers, and first responders are being priced out of the communities they serve. If we want to keep Hendricks County's workforce strong, we have to make it affordable to live here.
  • There are fewer than 300 income-based apartments for a county of 200,000 people. The gap between need and supply for affordable rentals is enormous — and federal cuts to HUD would make it catastrophically worse.
  • Property tax reform is a legitimate issue for homeowners worried about rising bills. But the answer isn't to defund schools or cut services — it's comprehensive housing policy that brings more supply onto the market at all price points.

4
Wages, Economic Anxiety & Tariff Uncertainty
State Federal

Hendricks County's average weekly wage of $1,061 in Q3 2025 ranked it among Indiana's larger counties — but still well below the national average of $1,459. Indiana as a whole ranked 36th in per capita real income in 2024, and the state's leading economic analysts have identified wages as Indiana's persistent weak point. The 2026 economic outlook for Indiana projects unemployment rising from 3.6% to 4.4%, as tariff-driven uncertainty slows manufacturing hiring. Nationally, nearly 400 tariff adjustments in 2025 alone created planning chaos for businesses, and Ball State's CBER economists warned of "mild stagflation" — slower growth with elevated prices. For Hendricks County's mix of logistics, warehousing, and commuter workers, this economic volatility lands directly on household budgets. The county's strong job market in logistics (Amazon, distribution centers) is partly offset by the fact that warehouse and logistics jobs often pay less and offer fewer benefits than the manufacturing jobs they replaced.

5
Healthcare Access & Medicaid Stability
State Federal

Indiana's Medicaid program — covering more than 1.4 million Hoosiers — is under simultaneous pressure from the state and federal level. The Healthy Indiana Plan (HIP), which covers low- to moderate-income adults, faces new work requirements effective July 1, 2026. The state is simultaneously re-bidding $68 billion in Medicaid managed care contracts (the "Mother of All Procurements"), with transitions not effective until 2029 and disruption already underway — 300,000 Hoosiers were told in November 2025 they had less than two months to switch insurers after MDwise was removed from the program. Federally, H.R. 1 (signed July 4, 2025) is projected to cut Indiana's Medicaid funding by 15% — an estimated $23 billion — between 2027 and 2036. Disability waiver slots, while partially reopened in July 2025, still cover only about one-third of the 13,000 Hoosiers on the waitlist for home- and community-based services. For HD-40 families with members who have disabilities, chronic illness, or limited income, Medicaid stability isn't an abstraction — it's the difference between independence and crisis.

  • There are 13,000 Hoosiers waiting for home and community-based disability services. Reopening some slots covered barely a third of them. For families with a child or loved one on that waitlist, this is a daily emergency, not a policy statistic.
  • Indiana's Medicaid rebid and work requirement changes are rolling out at the same time as federal cuts. That's a perfect storm for disruption — and the people who get hurt are among our most vulnerable neighbors.

6
Child Care Crisis & Working Family Stability
🔥 Active Crisis State Federal

Indiana's childcare funding crisis hits working families in fast-growing suburban counties like Hendricks especially hard. State CCDF (Child Care and Development Fund) reimbursement rates were cut up to 35% for school-age care in fall 2025. New low-income childcare vouchers were frozen — no new families added through at least 2026 — with more than 35,000 families statewide on a waitlist. On My Way Pre-K slots were cut by more than half statewide. In Hendricks County, where younger working families are moving in rapidly, affordable childcare is a direct workforce and economic issue: without it, parents — disproportionately mothers — are forced out of the labor market. Federal proposals to cut HUD and related social programs compound the pressure. For a county that prides itself on attracting young families and a strong workforce, letting childcare collapse is self-defeating economic policy.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Hendricks County is one of the fastest-growing counties in Indiana because families want to be here. But a family can't thrive in Avon — or anywhere else — if they can't find or afford childcare. The state is making that harder, not easier.
  • Childcare is workforce infrastructure. When the state cuts reimbursement rates by 35% and freezes new vouchers, it's not saving money — it's forcing parents out of jobs, shrinking the labor pool, and hurting local businesses that need workers.
  • More than 35,000 Indiana families are on the waitlist for childcare assistance. In a county where young families are moving in every month, we can't afford to tell them the state's answer to affordable childcare is "wait in line."

District & Race Context

What Makes HD-40 the Polar Anchor of This Regional Wave

HD-40 covers Avon in Hendricks County — Indiana's fastest-growing suburban county and a bellwether for how Indianapolis's western exurban ring thinks and votes. At a +12.4R IN-Index, it is the most reachable seat in the five-candidate Power Pack regional wave, and a strong Democratic showing here — even a near-miss — creates political legitimacy and organizing infrastructure that lifts every other candidate in the group. The incumbent, Gregory Steuerwald, holds the seat from Avon and has been in the House since 2008. HD-40 is part of CD-4.

The district is classic outer-suburb: homeowner-heavy, younger working families, high education attainment (37%+ with bachelor's degrees countywide), employed in logistics, healthcare, and Indianapolis-area professional sectors. These are voters who care deeply about their kids' schools, their commute, their property values, and economic stability — and who demonstrated in November 2025 that they will push back when Statehouse Republicans overreach into local school funding. The Lt. Gov.'s failed anti-referendum campaign in Avon is a powerful piece of recent local evidence that Republican overreach alienates even suburban conservative-leaning voters.

The key voter in HD-40 is a Avon homeowner with school-age children: they voted for the school referendum in November, they're watching their property taxes and home values closely, they commute on increasingly crowded roads, and they're beginning to feel the squeeze of a state government that tells them to "do more with less" while cutting education and healthcare funding. A candidate who connects economic dignity, school investment, and government accountability to their daily experience can make this race competitive. The question is organizing, candidate quality, and whether the regional wave has enough momentum to pull voters across the +12.4R gap.
