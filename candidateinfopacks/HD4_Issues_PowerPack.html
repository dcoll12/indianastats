District Identity

Porter County is Chicago's Indiana backyard — a Lake Michigan shoreline county defined by steel industry roots, the Indiana Dunes National Park, commuter rail access to Chicago, and a rapidly growing suburban population. It sits at the intersection of blue-collar industrial heritage and white-collar professional exurban growth. At +10.1R, this is the biggest stretch in the pack — but Porter County's political identity is shifting, and real local tensions around land use, corporate development, housing costs, and working-class economic anxiety are creating genuine openings.

Corporate Land Grab, Data Centers & Community Control
Over 1,000 Porter County residents said "County, Not Corporate" and stopped a $2B data center on farmland near a school. The fight isn't over.
🔥 Live Local ConflictLocalStateCommunity Control

In May 2025, more than 1,000 Union Township residents packed the Porter County Expo Center in Valparaiso to oppose a $2 billion data center campus proposed by QTS (Blackstone-owned) on nearly 800 acres of farmland near Wheeler High School. Residents raised concerns about energy usage, environmental impact, proximity to schools, and the lack of transparent communication from developers. They wore orange shirts with the slogan "Wheeler Country, Not Corporate." The Porter County commissioners ultimately moved away from the project. A separate Agincourt data center proposal in Valparaiso was also rejected earlier in the year. A 2,500-signature petition opposed another 467-acre data center proposal in Franklin Township. Porter County's Plan Commission and Board of Commissioners are still navigating ongoing pressure from hyperscale data center developers — and residents are watching closely. This is not anti-development sentiment; residents explicitly said the county wasn't "anti data center" — it was about siting, transparency, and who benefits.

The question is whether we have elected officials who will keep listening — or who will find ways to let developers back in through the side door.

Steel Industry, Tariffs & Working-Class Economic Anxiety
Targeted steel tariffs helped NW Indiana mills — but broader tariff volatility threatens the supply chains those same workers depend on.
📍 Core Identity IssueLocalFederal

Northwest Indiana's steel mills ring Lake Michigan, and Porter County is part of that industrial landscape. Indiana's steel industry grew from roughly 18,100 workers in 2017 to 22,390 by 2023, partly thanks to targeted steel tariffs that boosted prices and stabilized employment. Union Steelworkers District 7's director acknowledged that the tariffs "brought back workers who were laid off and facilities that were idled." But the situation is more complex in 2025–2026. Broader tariffs — 25% on Canada and Mexico — are raising costs for the steel mills themselves, which depend on imported inputs. Economists at IU Northwest cautioned that while steel producers benefit temporarily from higher prices, the broader tariff package erodes economic confidence and hits downstream manufacturers that use steel. The region is navigating a tension between the identity politics of tariff support and the economic reality that 400 tariff adjustments in 2025 alone are creating business uncertainty and slowing hiring. NW Indiana's 2026 forecast projects 1.8% GRP growth — modest and uneven.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Porter County's working families built this region with their hands — in the mills, on the docks, in the warehouses. They deserve trade policy that actually protects their jobs, not trade policy that's really about political optics while their employers absorb uncertainty and slow down hiring.
  • Targeted steel tariffs helped NW Indiana mills and we should support them. But 400 tariff adjustments in a single year? That's not a trade policy — that's chaos. Businesses can't plan, workers can't count on stable employment, and the supply chain that feeds Porter County's manufacturers gets squeezed from both ends.
  • The steel industry is Porter County's economic backbone — but it's also transitioning. Logistics, healthcare, and professional services are growing. We need state-level workforce investment that helps steelworkers build new skills when automation changes the mills, not just trade rhetoric that promises to turn back the clock.
  • Working-class families in Portage and Chesterton deserve elected officials who tell them the truth about trade: some tariffs help, broad tariff chaos hurts, and no politician in Washington can promise to bring back exactly the jobs that left decades ago. The honest answer is workforce investment, not slogans.
Sources: NWI Times, "Steel tariffs stabilized employment at mills but broader tariffs raise worries" (Mar. 2025); IU Business Research Center, NW Indiana 2026 Forecast; Ball State CBER Indiana Economic Outlook 2026.

Housing Affordability, Growth & Community Character
Porter County needs 50,000 more homes in 25 years — but residents are already pushing back on density, annexation, and sprawl. Smart growth or no growth is the tension.
LocalState

Porter County is a high-demand housing market straining under growth pressure from the Chicago metro. Residents in June 2025 packed Valparaiso City Hall to oppose annexation of 80 acres for 300-unit housing development — citing traffic density and infrastructure capacity. The NIRPC's NWI 2050+ plan projects Lake, Porter, and La Porte counties will need 50,000 more homes in the next 25 years. An IU Northwest housing study found that housing in the region is increasingly unaffordable: a household needs a median income of $83,000 to afford the median-priced home in Lake County (only 32% of households qualify), and similar dynamics apply across Porter County. A multi-faith Porter County Housing Assembly in May 2025 drew 200 residents focused on "Housing for All" — seniors, first-time buyers, renters, and the growing homeless population. One Valparaiso senior was on a waiting list for affordable independent senior housing for three years before finally getting in. The tension between affordable housing needs and neighborhood opposition to density is a defining local conflict.

Indiana Dunes, Environment & Federal Land Management
4.6 million visitors a year. A National Park on Porter County's doorstep. And a federal administration that has shown little interest in protecting public lands.
LocalFederal

The Indiana Dunes National Park is Porter County's crown jewel — attracting 4.6 million annual visitors and driving the county's tourism economy. Indiana Dunes Tourism, under the leadership of Christine Livingston, has been building the Dunes brand as a regional connector across all of Porter County. The Dunes also represent an environmental heritage that shapes Porter County's identity — 16% of properties in Porter County face severe flood risk over the next 30 years, and proximity to Lake Michigan makes climate-related water level changes a long-term concern. Residents who packed the data center hearings cited "environment and quality of life" as their core concerns. At the federal level, the Trump administration's approach to national park staffing, land management, and environmental regulation has created anxiety among communities — like Porter County — where the federal public land is an economic and cultural anchor. Porter County's "smart growth" identity depends on the Dunes staying protected.

South Shore Line, Transit Investment & the Chicago Commuter Economy
The South Shore Line double-track is complete. Chesterton has direct rail to Chicago. Now Porter County needs to grow around transit — not sprawl away from it.
LocalStateFederal

The South Shore Line's double-track project, completed in May 2024, added 14 weekday trains for more frequent service and reduced travel times between Porter County's stations and Chicago's Millennium Station. This investment — built over years of federal, state, and local partnership — is now reshaping Porter County's housing and development dynamics. Chesterton, Porter, Ogden Dunes and other communities along the Lakeshore Corridor have become increasingly attractive to Chicago-area professionals seeking more affordable housing with transit access. The NIRPC's NWI 2050+ plan calls for transit-oriented development — compact, walkable, mixed-use neighborhoods centered around train stations — as the key to meeting housing demand without sprawl. A Valparaiso extension of the South Shore Line has been studied and proposed in the Regional Development Authority's 2025 strategic plan. Federal transit funding — which the Trump administration has shown interest in cutting — is what made the double-track and Monon Corridor possible.

Candidate Talking Points
  • The South Shore double-track is one of the best investments in Porter County in a generation. Fourteen more trains per day means more options for workers, less congestion on the highways, and more economic activity along the corridor. That's what public investment looks like when it works.
  • A Valparaiso South Shore branch is in the regional plan. That would transform downtown Valparaiso's connection to the Chicago economy — bringing jobs, investment, and housing demand that the city can shape through smart transit-oriented development. We should be fighting for that, not waiting.

It catalyzes affordable housing development near stations, reduces car dependency for families who can't afford multiple vehicles, and makes communities more accessible for seniors and people with disabilities. Property Taxes, Opioid Recovery & Local Government Capacity
Property taxes are the #1 reason Porter County residents visit the county website. SEA 1's impacts are landing now — and opioid settlement funds need smart stewardship.
StateLocal

Property tax issues are the single largest reason Porter County residents visit the county's website — nearly 60% of traffic. Indiana's SEA 1 (2025 property tax reform) is restructuring the homestead deduction framework through 2031, with complex implications for Porter County homeowners and the local government revenue that funds roads, schools, and emergency services. Porter County's own legislative brief from September 2025 laid out the impacts of SEA 1 on assessed values and tax revenue. Separately, Porter County has established an Opioid Crisis Committee to manage and distribute opioid settlement grant funds to local nonprofits, healthcare providers, and community coalitions — an active, community-led recovery investment. Porter County Prosecutor Gary Germann's office is explicitly centered on programs that "lift people up" beyond just convictions. These local government functions — property tax administration, recovery investment, public safety — are exactly what voters in a +10R district think about government doing well or poorly.

Candidate Talking Points
  • Property taxes are the most personal thing Porter County government does — nearly 60% of residents who visit the county website are there because of a tax question. When the state reforms the system, Porter County families and local government feel it directly. We need legislators who understand those downstream effects, not just the Statehouse politics.

District & Race Context

What Makes HD-4 the Northwest Indiana Polar Anchor

At +10.1R, HD-4 is the hardest pickup in this pack. Ed Soliday (R) holds the seat from Valparaiso and has not faced a serious Democratic challenge in years. Ryan Kominakis is running as a Democrat in a district that voted 10 points more Republican than the state average. This race requires cross-persuasion of culturally Republican but economically and community-values-driven voters — not base mobilization.

The opening here is real, but narrow. Porter County's political identity is complex: it has steel union households that historically voted Dem and now lean Republican; it has college-educated Chicago transplants who lean Democratic; and it has a growing exurban population that is politically volatile on local issues. The QTS data center fight revealed something important: Porter County residents will organize and fight back against corporate overreach even in a deep-red county. That "County, Not Corporate" energy is politically untethered — it's neither Democrat nor Republican, it's communitarian. A candidate who can speak to that value authentically, and connect it to broader questions of economic dignity, housing, and environmental stewardship, can build a coalition.

The South Shore Line double-track is a tangible example of what public investment produces — and federal transit funding threats give a Democratic candidate a concrete contrast. Steel tariff complexity creates an opening to speak honestly to working-class voters about trade policy rather than just waving the flag. And the housing affordability crisis is affecting people across the political spectrum in Porter County — seniors who can't afford to age in place, young families priced out, renters paying too much.

The key voter in HD-4 is not a base Democrat. They're a Porter County homeowner — possibly a steelworker, a South Shore commuter, a Valparaiso small business owner, or a longtime resident who fought the data center — who is skeptical of both corporate power and government overreach, proud of their community, and worried about the direction of things. A candidate who listens, speaks plainly, and demonstrates they'll fight for Porter County's character — not for a party — has a path.
